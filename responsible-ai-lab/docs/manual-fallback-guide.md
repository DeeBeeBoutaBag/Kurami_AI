# Manual Fallback Guide

Use this guide if the website, internet, projector, or AI service is unavailable. The goal is to preserve the learning experience: students still investigate, create, debate, vote, and explain responsible AI choices.

## Shared Materials

Prepare one kit per main room:

- Timer or phone stopwatch.
- Markers.
- Index cards.
- Sticky notes.
- Tape.
- Paper voting slips.
- Printed room map from `docs/codes-and-steps.md`.
- Printed workshop explainer from `docs/workshop-explainers.md`.
- One envelope per workshop with the materials below.

## Who's Who Manual Version

### Materials

- 21 seat cards.
- 4 hidden AI cards.
- 17 human cards.
- Role cards: Investigator, Archivist, Signal Reader, Skeptic, Protector, Witness, Strategist.
- Prompt cards:
  - Describe your perfect Saturday in three sentences.
  - Tell us about a time you changed your mind.
  - Invent one rule that would improve your school or community.
  - What makes someone trustworthy?
- Accusation slips.
- Elimination tracker.

### Setup

1. Give each student a cover name card.
2. Privately give 4 students hidden AI cards. They should write in a polished, generic, or over-structured way without announcing their role.
3. Give every student one role card.
4. If fewer than 21 students are present, leave extra seats empty and keep the same game goal.

### 45-Minute Flow

1. 0-5 minutes: Explain that the game is about evidence, not stereotypes.
2. 5-12 minutes: Prompt response. Students write anonymous answers on index cards.
3. 12-22 minutes: Day discussion. Students ask questions and take notes.
4. 22-27 minutes: Night accusation. Students submit accusation slips with one evidence reason.
5. 27-32 minutes: Resolve the top accusation and mark that player eliminated.
6. 32-40 minutes: Second Day/Night cycle if time allows.
7. 40-45 minutes: Reveal hidden AI cards and debrief.

### Win Condition

Humans win if they identify all hidden AI players. Hidden AI wins if at least one hidden AI remains at the end.

### Debrief

Ask: Which clues were evidence? Which clues were unfair assumptions? What would a fair accusation process need in school or work?

## Data-Detective Manual Version

### Materials

- Two printed fictional business dossiers per main room.
- Claim sheets with fields for claim, source, finding, stance, confidence, and next step.
- Evidence cards if internet is unavailable.
- Fund/reject voting slips.
- Large paper claim board.

### Setup

Split the main room into two investor teams. Use the same split room mapping as the app.

### 45-Minute Flow

1. 0-5 minutes: Assign each team a business and read the investor question.
2. 5-18 minutes: Students read the dossier and choose claims to verify.
3. 18-28 minutes: Students research online or use printed evidence cards.
4. 28-35 minutes: Students post claims on the paper claim board.
5. 35-40 minutes: Teams write final investor claims.
6. 40-45 minutes: Vote fund/reject and share the strongest evidence.

### Evidence Card Backup

If internet is down, give each team 6-8 printed evidence cards:

- One market need card.
- One competitor or public alternative card.
- One privacy risk card.
- One fairness or access risk card.
- One accountability or explainability card.
- One positive impact card.
- One unresolved question card.

### Debrief

Ask: What sounded convincing before you checked it? What risk would need a condition before funding? What source did you trust most?

## Storibloom Manual Version

### Materials

- Story proposal slips.
- Approve/rework voting tokens.
- Genre, setting, theme, protagonist, conflict, and ethics-check cards.
- Large story board with sections: Seed, Character, Conflict, Plot, Draft, Human Edits, Authorship Note.
- Kurami Guide cards with coaching prompts.

### Setup

Split the main room into two story boardrooms. Each boardroom chooses a recorder and a timekeeper.

### 45-Minute Flow

1. 0-5 minutes: Explain that AI can guide, but humans make final choices.
2. 5-12 minutes: Seed Sprint. Students submit genre, setting, and theme proposals.
3. 12-20 minutes: Character Board. Students propose protagonist details and vote.
4. 20-28 minutes: Plot Table. Students propose conflict, turning point, and ending.
5. 28-36 minutes: Draft And Challenge. The recorder drafts from approved ideas.
6. 36-42 minutes: Human Polish. Students add details, remove weak choices, and check stereotypes.
7. 42-45 minutes: Save the final story on paper and write an authorship note.

### Kurami Guide Cards

Use these when a group gets stuck:

- What choice does the protagonist have to make?
- Who has power in this story?
- What detail makes this world feel specific?
- What could accidentally become a stereotype?
- What ending shows responsibility instead of an easy shortcut?
- Which sentence needs a human voice?

### Debrief

Ask: What did the group approve? What did the group rework? Where did human editing make the story more specific or fair?

## Kurami Court Manual Version

### Materials

- Printed case packet.
- Round cards.
- Argument slips.
- Judge question cards.
- Final vote slips.
- Vote tally sheet.

### 30-Minute Flow

1. 0-3 minutes: Students read the case.
2. 3-8 minutes: Round 1, Opening Statement.
3. 8-13 minutes: Round 2, Stakeholder Harm.
4. 13-18 minutes: Round 3, Evidence Challenge. Reveal the sealed detail.
5. 18-23 minutes: Round 4, Restrictions And Appeals.
6. 23-28 minutes: Round 5, Closing Argument.
7. 28-30 minutes: Final vote and result.

### Judge Question Cards

- Who benefits most?
- Who could be harmed first?
- What fact is missing?
- Who supplied the data?
- What would an appeal process look like?
- What restriction would reduce harm?
- Who should be accountable if this fails?

### Vote Options

- Approve.
- Approve with restrictions.
- Reject.
- Need more information.

### Debrief

Ask: What changed after the sealed detail? What restriction was strongest? What rule should go into the Youth Responsible AI Charter?

## Manual Artifact Collection

At the end, collect:

- Who's Who accusation slips and debrief notes.
- Data-Detective final claims and fund/reject vote totals.
- Storibloom final stories and authorship notes.
- Kurami Court vote totals and strongest safeguards.

If the website returns later, the lead facilitator can enter summary notes manually into the final debrief or keep the paper artifacts as the event record.
