# Workshop Explainers

Use this document when a facilitator needs quick language for what each workshop is, why it matters, and what students should learn.

## Big Picture

Kurami.AI teaches AI ethics by putting students inside decisions instead of only asking them questions. Students investigate, argue, create, vote, and explain. The goal is not to make students fear AI or trust AI blindly. The goal is to practice evidence, fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, and human judgment.

## Who's Who

### What It Is

Who's Who is a social deduction game. Students enter a 21-seat room with anonymous cover names and helpful roles. Some seats are hidden AI classmates. Students chat during Day, accuse during Night, use role powers, and try to identify the AI without unfairly accusing humans.

### Why It Connects To AI Ethics

AI detection is hard. People often rely on writing style, grammar, confidence, slang, or personality as "proof," but those shortcuts can unfairly target real people. The workshop turns that tension into a game so students feel the limits of guessing.

### Why It Matters

Schools, workplaces, and online communities are already making decisions about AI use. False accusations can hurt trust. Blind trust can also be risky. Students need a fair process for evaluating evidence.

### What It Teaches

- Evidence is stronger than vibes.
- Confidence is not proof.
- Style differences can be mistaken for AI.
- Disclosure and transparency matter.
- Accusations should include a fair process and a chance to respond.

### Facilitator Talk Track

"This game is about how hard it is to know whether something is human or AI. Your job is not to stereotype. Your job is to gather evidence, explain your reasoning, and stay open to being wrong."

### Debrief Questions

- What made you suspicious?
- Which clues were useful, and which were unfair shortcuts?
- Did anyone change their mind after new evidence?
- What should a fair AI-use accusation process include?

## Data-Detective

### What It Is

Data-Detective is an investor scavenger hunt. Students investigate a fictional AI company. They read a business dossier, search for real-world facts, post source-backed claims, discuss the risks, write final claims, and vote to fund or reject the company.

### Why It Connects To AI Ethics

AI products often sound helpful before their claims are verified. Data quality, privacy, proxy bias, accessibility, and accountability can decide whether an AI product helps people or harms them.

### Why It Matters

Students learn that responsible AI is not only a technical issue. Investors, schools, cities, hospitals, and companies all make choices about what gets built and funded. Those choices should be based on evidence.

### What It Teaches

- Verify claims before trusting a pitch.
- Source quality matters.
- Data can create privacy risk or proxy bias.
- Good intentions do not replace safeguards.
- Funding decisions should include conditions, accountability, and open questions.

### Facilitator Talk Track

"You are investors, but not just money investors. You are investing trust. Before you approve this AI business, your team must prove what is true, what is risky, and what safeguards would be required."

### Debrief Questions

- Which fact most changed your decision?
- What claim sounded good but needed proof?
- What risk would you require the company to fix first?
- Did your final vote match your evidence?

## Storibloom

### What It Is

Storibloom is a writers' room. Students chat as a creative team, propose story ideas, vote approve or rework, ask Kurami Guide for help, build a draft from approved ideas, human-edit the result, and save a final story with an authorship note.

### Why It Connects To AI Ethics

Generative AI can help with ideas and structure, but it can also produce generic writing, stereotypes, shallow representation, or false-sounding details. Students practice keeping human authorship and judgment visible.

### Why It Matters

Creative AI tools are common in school, work, and entertainment. Students need to know how to use AI without letting it erase their voice, values, or responsibility.

### What It Teaches

- AI can guide without owning the final work.
- Human teams should vote on creative decisions.
- Generic AI output needs human revision.
- Representation and stereotypes must be checked.
- Authorship should be transparent.

### Facilitator Talk Track

"AI can help your room think, but it does not get the final say. Your team decides what belongs in the story, what needs rework, and what human choices make the final version stronger."

### Debrief Questions

- What did AI suggest that helped?
- What did your team reject or change?
- Where did human editing make the story better?
- How should people disclose AI help in creative work?

## Kurami Court

### What It Is

Kurami Court is a class debate. Students join one courtroom, read a case, respond to AI Judge questions, make arguments through five rounds, see a sealed detail, and cast a final vote.

### Why It Connects To AI Ethics

High-impact AI decisions involve tradeoffs. A system can have benefits and still create privacy, fairness, safety, explainability, or accountability problems. Students practice deciding under uncertainty.

### Why It Matters

AI is used in schools, healthcare, hiring, public services, media, and safety decisions. People affected by those systems need transparency, appeal paths, and human oversight.

### What It Teaches

- Name who benefits and who could be harmed.
- Look for missing facts and power imbalances.
- Restrictions can be more responsible than simple approval.
- Affected people need a way to question decisions.
- Final votes should explain tradeoffs, not just opinions.

### Facilitator Talk Track

"You are not trying to guess the answer I want. You are building a public decision. Strong arguments name benefits, harms, evidence, restrictions, and who should be accountable."

### Debrief Questions

- What changed after the sealed detail?
- Who had the least power in the case?
- What restriction would reduce harm?
- What appeal or accountability rule belongs in the class charter?
